Increased Economic Equity and Support

Increased Internship Grant Opportunities

As we prepare to enter a highly competitive post-graduation job market, we each deserve the opportunity to gain experiential knowledge through participation in summer internship programs. Tufts should incorporate internship grants into its major fundraising goals so that all students can partake and learn from these valuable experiences.

More Accessible Career Services Resources

We deserve a Career Services Department that will help guide us through the confusing processes of applying for private grants and loans for jobs and internship programs. Additionally, Career Services should extend application deadlines for summer internship grants, as most students do not secure employment until April or May. We should continue working in partnership with alumni to find more unique opportunities for networking and career mentorship.

More Transparent Financial Aid Processes

The financial aid application process should be more transparent and student-friendly. If Tufts cuts or reduces your aid, you deserve a full, understandable explanation. We must work to reform the current process into a more interactive model. Increased opportunities to consult with financial representatives about aid packages and loans will help students make informative decisions about living off campus and other expenses.

Optional Financial Literacy Workshops

As a period of newfound independence, our years at Tufts represent a prime opportunity to learn more about personal financing and responsible budgeting and spending. Tufts should offer optional workshops during freshman orientation and throughout the year so that students may learn and fully understand valuable lessons like how to budget a checking account, understand a credit score, or take out a personal loan.

Expanded Work-Study Programs

We should expand beyond the traditional work-study information sessions held during orientation so that students of every year can have the opportunity to meet with councilors each semester as they hunt for jobs on campus. Additionally, we should look into expanding the Tufts work-study program so that students may earn their money by working internships or other career-oriented activities in the Medford/Somerville area.

Need Blind Admissions Policy

Tufts currently labels itself as a “need sensitive” institution. We must continue working to implement a need-blind admissions policy, and take all necessary steps to admit the most dynamic and competitive classes of applicants.

Financial Assistance for Student Activities Fees

We all know firsthand that college is expensive. Given that many students work throughout the school year and often manage tight personal budgets, we must reduce costs as much as possible. We should establish a fund with unused TCU surplus money to support students struggling to afford registration or participation fees for club sports, student groups, and academic conferences. This past year, Senate rolled-over tens of thousands of dollars, there is room for us to help students do more.

Strengthened and Expanded Academic Structures So Students Can Fully Pursue Their Passions

Campus Innovation and Entrepreneurial Leadership Studies Major

Recently ranked one of the most innovative campuses in the greater Boston area, Tufts must offer more academic and extracurricular resources to feed the creative minds of our student body. We should push for more courses in finance and social entrepreneurship, as well as the creation of an Entrepreneurial Leadership Studies major. We must also create collaborative learning spaces that support the inventive and entrepreneurial spirit of our campus, just as our peers at MIT and Harvard have on their campuses.

Teacher Registration and Education Major

Currently, Tufts students can only become certified to teach Kindergarten-2nd grade through the Child Development major. Utilizing the resources of the Eliot Pearson Children’s School (a Tufts graduate school that offers a Masters in Teaching) and our partnerships with the Medford, Somerville, and Boston Public School Systems, we should work to create an Education major that helps students participate in fieldwork and earn their teacher’s certification for primary and secondary school teaching.

American Sign Language and the Language Requirement

Currently, students cannot count American Sign Language classes for Part 1 of the Tufts language requirement. This policy should change to formally recognize ASL as a proper language and credit students for their academic efforts.

Hindi and Korean Language Course Offerings

In response to high student demand, Tufts should offer Hindi and Korean courses as part of its world languages curriculum. These academic opportunities will prove valuable assets for many students who plan to pursue careers based in Eastern and Southern Asia.

Strengthened Critical Studies in Disparities and Diasporas (C2D) and Women, Gender, and Sexuality Studies (WGS) Programs

In the wake of the recent creation of an Africana Studies major and minor as well as an Asian-American Studies minor, it is now critical that students who wish to specialize in the areas have access to the resources necessary for academic scholarship. We must hire new faculty in order to develop new course offerings that will fill in existing gaps in the curriculum. Additionally, the Working Group responsible for guiding the C2D Program must welcome student feedback and opinion as curriculum decisions are made. While strengthening the existing C2D areas of study, the Working Group must solicit research and lay groundwork for the incorporation of other areas of study including Latino Studies and Native American Studies.

The newly renamed Women, Gender, and Sexuality Studies program (formerly Women’s Studies) must also receive more institutional support. Moving forward, we must begin to lay groundwork for the incorporation of more Queer Studies course offerings.

Re-Focusing of the World Civilizations Requirement

We should re-focus the World Civilizations Requirement to encompass Tufts’ educational pillars of Active and Global citizenship. This would expand the current amount of acceptable courses to include those that focus on identity and cultural difference in a domestic setting, allowing students strengthen their academic profile and take courses related to their specific majors.

More Efficient Academic Bureaucracy

Nearly all of us have at some point grown frustrated with the academic bureaucracy at Tufts. We deserve more easily navigable processes that will increase the efficiency of our course registration and selection. As Tufts continues to test the new ISIS program, traditional course booklets should be made available in print and online copy. The TCU Senate should continue its efforts to publicize all syllabi and course evaluations online so that students may make informed decisions when creating their academic schedules. Information about courses and cross-registration opportunities at other universities should become more publicized around campus. We should extend the add/drop deadline of classes for upperclassmen and require that professors provide each student with some sort of grade or feedback before this date.

More Personalized First Year Orientation Program

We have all experienced freshman orientation and know firsthand the overwhelming craziness that consumes the week. In order to facilitate a smoother transition into college life, we should develop a more personalized and interactive approach to orientation. Both lighthearted games and more serious conversations about mental health or sexual assault should solicit student involvement in a personalized and interactive fashion that incorporates every voice and perspective in a more intimate setting. Orientation programming should extend throughout freshman year, introducing new Jumbos to extracurricular activities on campus and cultural opportunities in Boston.

Administrative Policies That Reflect a Real Understanding of Student Body

Medical Amnesty Alcohol Policy

We deserve an alcohol policy that prioritizes safety over punishment. We need a Medical Amnesty Policy – a Good Samaritan Policy doesn’t go far enough. When someone’s life is in danger because they over-indulged in alcohol, there cannot be any deterrence to calling for help. By threatening probation, we are putting punishment over students’ lives. We must establish a Medical Amnesty Policy that forgives the student who calls TEMS as well as the student who drank too much. It’s time for us to put students first, before punishments and risky policies.

Strong and Stable Non-Discrimination Policy

Discrimination by any other name is still discrimination. We deserve a strong and stable university-wide non-discrimination policy that protects members of our community and does not include any loopholes. We must work to modify the current policy that allows student groups to deviate from the code by adhering to certain criteria.

Fair and Decriminalized Marijuana Policy

Last November, Massachusetts voters passed a referendum that approved the prescription and use of medical marijuana throughout the commonwealth. During this period of transition, Tufts must work with state government and other relevant agencies to craft university policy that allows students in need who meet specified legal criteria to use medical marijuana on campus. We deserve a campus drug policy that reflects the decriminalization of marijuana in Massachusetts. The university must work more transparently as they coordinate with state and federal authorities to execute a drug policy that adheres to state law.

Reformed Sexual Assault Policy

In order to reform the punitive model with which they are currently handled, sexual assault cases should be dealt with by the Office of Equal Opportunity (not the Dean of Students Office) to create a safer space for assault survivors on campus. Professional advocates should be hired to represent both sides of a reported case so that each person may cohesively navigate university bureaucracy with the assistance of a personal caseworker. A centralized website must also be created that will help survivors connect with mental and physical health resources as well as report an assault to the proper authorities on campus. At least one confidential and non-mandated reporter should live in each dorm on campus so that students may discuss their options in a welcoming and nonjudgmental setting. The university must also alter how it defines and interprets rape to incorporate and reflect the experiences faced during many same-sex assaults. Finally, the university must change current rules that place survivors on probation who take lees than three classes a semester while seeking physical or mental health services on or around campus.

Well Defined Free Speech Policy

We deserve a stable, well-defined, and informative freedom of speech policy that defends each student’s right to free expression while simultaneously establishing protections to effectively address hate and assaultive speech. We must proactively address this issue and incorporate many opinions from all parts of campus.

Protection of Campus Workers and Staff

Each person who works at Tufts deserves respect and dignity as a member of our community. As we continue to institute new projects on campus, we must ensure that the administration is held accountable in paying fair wages and respectable working conditions.

Environmental Sustainability

We deserve an environmentally sustainable university that continually works to reduce its negative impact on our fragile climate. We must continue investing in alternative energy sources as part of future campus construction projects and create a more centralized and institutionalized system for composting. We must ensure that we invest our endowment in socially and environmentally responsible companies.

A More Representative and Responsive TCU Senate

Increasing Outreach

We deserve a TCU Senate that truly understands our needs as a student body. However, many Senators remain disconnected from the Tufts community at large. Senate should invite more student leaders to its meetings and collaborate with groups on resolutions and projects that relate to their specific goals and issues. Senators must attend student group meetings and engage the Tufts community to learn more about issues faced by their constituencies.

Taking a Stand

The work of Senate often appears tragically irrelevant to most students. Though Senate has the potential to work as an effective agent for change on campus, it often refuses to engage controversial issues. We deserve a Senate that will engage in respectful, thoughtful, and educated debate about difficult topics. We also deserve a student government that has the courage to seek truth and advocate for a more equitable Tufts community.

Social Opportunities through Collaboration

Increased Coordination with Programming Board

Senate must strengthen its relationship with Programming Board to promote and financially support more events on campus that engage the entire student body. Additionally, more weekend programming should be offered that provides a fun, sober alternative for students who do not wish to participate in drinking culture on campus.

Socializing with Other Schools through NESCAC and Boston Intercollegiate Assembly

Utilizing our common bonds with other colleges and universities, we should collaborate to create new and exciting opportunities for students from both of our campuses to socialize and connect. With other NESCAC schools, we can facilitate social programming around athletic weekends that could include tailgating and game attendance as well as nighttime social events like dances. With members of the Boston Intercollegiate Assembly, we can work to host events that will engage students from colleges throughout the Boston area. These events will provide students with awesome opportunities to connect and form friendships with people from other local schools.

Lobbying for Student Discounts and Extended T Service

We should collaborate with our partner schools in the Boston Intercollegiate Assembly to continue lobbying for student discounts to museums, shows, and sporting events in Boston. Additionally, we should use this network to lobby for extension of the T’s operating hours on the weekends so that students may travel to and from the city in a safe and efficient manner.

Incentivized Co-sponsorship

Senate should implement more financial incentives that encourage student groups to cosponsor events on campus.

Accessible Fleet of Vans

Senate should continue its project to create a university-wide fleet of vans that can be used by all students groups on campus.

Supporting Our Jumbo Athletes

Senate should collaborate with and financially support the initiatives of Tufts Spirit Coalition and Fan the Fire to increase attendance at campus sporting events. Scheduled night games on campus will also provide a fun social outlet for many students, especially those who wish to remain sober on the weekends.

A Safer and More Welcoming Community

Expansion of Gender Neutral Housing

Students should be able to choose whomever they want they want to live with on campus, regardless of gender difference. We should expand the current optional gender-neutral housing program to all dorms on campus, including Hillsides Apartments and Latin Way suites. We should also build gender-neutral bathrooms around campus, especially in highly trafficked public spaces like the Campus Center, Dewick, and Carmichael dining halls.

Increased Opportunities to Live on Campus

After this year’s recent housing debacle, we must work with to ensure that all students who wish to can live on campus. Working with Res Life, we must assess the current popularity of on-campus housing for upperclassmen and begin exploring the possibility of increasing on-campus living spaces through dorm renovation or new construction projects.

Reformed Role of RAs

We all know that Tufts students drink. In order to ensure the health and safety of everyone, we must take necessary steps to ensure a culture of responsible drinking on campus. We should restructure the current role of RA’s from that of “policing” to “peer mentorship.” The guidance of caring, trustworthy, and responsible upperclassmen in each dorm, each acting outside the a friend and not a symbol of university sanction and punishment, would encourage the development of a social scene that encourages personal well being over punishment. These RA’s would prove an indispensible asset for all students on campus, especially those who do not drink before entering college.

Increased Physical Accessibility Across Campus

As we pursue renovation and construction projects our campus each year, we must consider physical accessibility a majority priority. It is essential that we build ramps, smooth street curves, and soften inclines to ease the movement of all, especially the physically handicapped.

Common and Collaborative Social Spaces

In continuing efforts of past TCU Senates, we must work to create more common social spaces on campus for students to study, converse, and relax. With the closure of the RezQuad Café last year, hang out spots on campus have grown more and more scarce. We must protect these spaces and look to create new ones with twenty-four hour access that can foster academic collaboration and social connection.

Safer Intersections and Sidewalks

Over the past year, we have witnessed a severe need for safer intersections on and around campus. We must continue working with the Medford and Somerville city governments to promote street and sidewalk safety, paying special attention to the area near Powderhouse Circle.

Connecting with Neighborhoods Off Campus

Many upperclassmen decide to live off campus during junior or senior year. As they settle into their new houses, these students deserve access to important information about neighborhood rules and regulations, voting and local elections, and community resources and discounts. Working with the Medford and Somerville city governments and Tufts administration, we should develop more efficient methods of informing students about their new neighborhoods and communities. We should also host social events at the beginning of each school year to help foster community among Tufts students and their residential neighbors.

Humanist Chaplaincy

In addition to the many religious groups on campus, Tufts also boasts a vibrant atheist, agnostic, and non-religious community. To provide necessary resources for these students, we should work to bring a Humanist Chaplain to campus.

The Group of Six

The Group of Six Centers provide vital resources for many people on our campus. Open and welcoming to all students, these centers offer social, academic, and career-networking opportunities that add to the robust vibrancy of our community. We must continue to financially support these centers and ensure their stability for future years. We must also seek to provide resources to students who may not be able to turn to an already established center, including disabled students, Native American students, and students of Arab or Middle Eastern descent.

Greek Life

Greek Life serves a vital role in fostering community for many students and hosting social events throughout the year. To ensue the strength of these organizations, we must work for a smooth transition as another sorority chapter is established on campus next fall. We must also ease financial burdens faced by these groups. For Greek houses owned by the university, the Tufts administration should pay all property taxes and not force the burden on the Greek chapters themselves. We must push for the creation and inclusion of multicultural Greek organizations on our campus to provide more options for interested students.
